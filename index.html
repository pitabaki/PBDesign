Layout and Cover design for the book "Operating Manual for Spaceship Earth" by Buckminster Fuller. The process of designing the layout and cover of the book started with my first read of Bucky's masterpiece. For the design, I was inspired by his environmental consciousness, and his minimalistic nature. With that said, this is anything but minimalistic. However, I took his words and interpretted them as a return to analog, or a simpler, no waste lifestyle. Once I derived that meaning, I began experimenting with print making and texturing techniques to come up with an overall style.

All photos used within the book were taken by me using a Canon XTI.

For this project, I wanted to highlight some facts about the expansive nature of Starbucks, and the growing coffee culture of the world.

Before animating the video, a great deal of pre-production went into play. I started off with researching about Starbucks. I looked through several articles and documents, before deciding on what information I wanted to highlight. Afterwards, I began to storyboard— I highlighted key scenes and illustrated possible transitions that could playout within the animation. After created a solid storyline, with visuals, I opened up illustrator and began creating some layout comps. These comps defined my style, which brought me to the next stage: animation.

Whilst I was researching and creating graphics, I also recorded and mixed the music played throughout the video. I came up with the tune while playing my guitar, and immediately thought it would work as ambient sound. I worked on the mixing after recording, and paired it with some drum patterns and accent sounds.

Street Talk is a program, and system, that I came up with to combat the ever growing occurrence of parking tickets in the City/County of San Francisco.

For this project, I first researched about the problem. I combed through documentation after documentation about the monetary system behind the ticketing program, which included simulating a worst-case scenario (which is receiving a boot and being towed off). From that, I concluded
there truly is a problem (over 1.5 million tickets handed out annually), and the main culprit is
Alternate-Side Parking tickets — tickets doled out for parking in a street sweeping zone with street sweeping in effect.

That information opened up my mind to more avenues for research, and allowed me to create a research plan that could be utilized in attaining important information pertinent to my project. In this case, I found gathering information from drivers, via a survey, was a great way to move forward. From my surveys, interviews, and further research, I deduced that San Franciscans receive more parking tickets in violation of Alternate-Side Parking for the very same reason they forget Geometric formulas important for finding the volumetric properties of a trapezoid: it's just not part of their daily routine, or habit.

Afterwards, I researched what tools, apps, and programs were in play to assist in this problem.
While there are plenty of apps out there that'll assist in metered parking — apps that'll let you know
which meters are available to park at, and for how much to park at they cost — only a handful of apps that I found useful exist.

Because I felt an app only solves a portion of the problem, I decided to design a whole system. So here it is: Street Talk, a system partially built around a community of Good Samaritans and partially built around an automated reminder.

“'This Aint' Rocket Science” is an app that mixes the best parts of astronomy with the dreaded task of learning math. It, the app, aims to ease the user into learning both astronomy and math by interspersing lessons inbetween minigames, which then act as a breather for the brain.

The user embarks on missions to learn about our solar system. They start off at the beginning of our Sun-centric solar system, Mercury, and gain fuel as they answer questions correctly. They attain badges after successfully completing the minigames, and refuel their rocket everytime they answer a trivia question (or solve some other puzzle) correctly.

The goal of the game is to explore our entire solar system, while achieving an ever-higher score in all of the math and science portions.

Wedding Invitations

Final Deliverables

Invitation Layout

Design Process

Asset Sketches

For Seth and Erin’s wedding, I was asked to make invitations that would be mailed out to all of the guests. Unbeknownst to me, what I created here for the invitations would eventually become something of a branding campaign for the wedding. As an example of what I mean, guests were given cards with the ribbon in this design printed on it; When guests left for the evening, they were given coasters and beer koozies with my rocket design as the graphical feature. But I digress.

For this wedding invitation, I found inspiration from Seth and Erin, and their own interests, which include astronomy, tattoos, and books. I started my designs in a usual fashion, with a pencil and some paper. From there, I moved onto a solid trace with pen. My illustrations were then scanned into my computer, then traced and given color. The software used for this particular project was Adobe Illustrator and Photoshop.
